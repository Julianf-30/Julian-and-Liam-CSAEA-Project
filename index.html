How All the pieces move

Chess piece How it moves
Pawn Forward 1 space(option for two in opening), captures diagonally
Bishop Diagonally, any amount of spaces
Knight 2 spaces one way, perpindicular another way (L shape)
Rook Left, right, forward, backward any amount
Queen All directions any amount
King One space at at time all directions

Important Terms:

  • Check: Your king is under immediate attack by your opponents piece.
  • Checkmate: Your king is in check, and there is no way to get out of it
  • Fork: Two or more of your pieces are under attack and you can only defend one
  • Clock: The amount of time you have left to make a move.
  • En Passant: You can take your opponents pawn while on the same rank
  • Ranks and Files: Ranks are rows, files are columns
  • Castle: Your king and rook meet in the middle of one side of a rank and swap
  • Pin: You use a piece to pin one of your opponents pieces to another
  • Blunder: The move is a mistake and will probably result in the loss of a piece or worse position

Basic Rules of Chess

  1. White starts and then black goes.
  2. The goal of the game is to checkmate your opponent
  3. Running out of time results in a loss
  4. You can offer a draw to your opponent
  5. When you are white, queen gets the light square and the black queen gets the dark square
  6. Pawns that reach the end of the board must promote to any other piece- even if the piece has already been captured

Chess pieces:

Top of the board: Pawns. Bottom: Rook, Knight, Bishop, Queen, King

Parts of a chess game

The Opening

The parts of a chess game are the opening, middle game, and end game. There are tactics for all three. For starters, there are different types of openings. Some of them include: The Caro Kann, King's pawn, Queen's pawn, and Sicillian. The goal of the opening is to develop all of your pieces and get them off of their starting squares. You could also play a gambit. A gambit is when you sacrifice material for long term gain like a better position, or the idea of winning it back later with more gain. Gambits can be risky though, so they are not always played.


The Middle Game

During the middle game, the goal is to try and take advantage of your postion and your opponents postion. This is where you make trades, (capturing your oppenents piece for the price of a piece the same value). This is also where you try and attack your opponent with strategies. The middle game should set you up for a good end game, if played correctly. There is a saying that goes: "The perfect game of chess ends in a draw".


The End Game

During an endgame, you and your opponent will typically have not a lot of pieces, and one person usually has more. This is where pawn promotion can come into play. It is also where the winning player tries to checkmate their opponent and the losing player will try to defend, or flip the position to their advantage. Anything can happen during an end game, so your strategies need to be strong. Stalemate also happens during an end game. Stalemate is when the position is a draw because your king or your opponents king has no squares to move to. Be careful of this when going for checkmate.
